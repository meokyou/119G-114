Invisible Man

Ralph Waldo Ellison

  • Originally published on Monday, April 14, 2022
  • The publisher is—presently—Penguin Random House LLC (formerly Random House and Penguin Group)

    • This book is about a person back in the 70s that had to suffer through racism and to entertain all these white men who were manipulating his life like a marionette gets controlled by a puppet-master. This poor boy that grows up to become a man in a much shorter time than most people has it harder than most people, this makes him as a result much tougher than most people causing him to do whatever is necessary to achieve the goal of a civil rights movement to continue the dream for equity. Vivid scenes of violence permeate the book's composition contributing to the idea, that feeling of sickening discomfort, of confusioned, frienzed progression, such as the narrator's own unfair, unforgiving biographic. The many unsavoury encounters that the narrator is drowned in during his bildungsroman allows us the reader to better comprehend his adversity to depart his well-lit, yet drab, escapism-rendered, underground abode. We are introduced to this in the prolouge, which only creates intrigue over his eventual exodus, perhaps—only parially—representing his truma-inspired languid conclusion which we are privy to only within the final pages. Of course, the state the world he hid from, , is macabre as riots assault the streets of Harlem.

National Museum of Civil Rights

The National Civil Rights Museum is a complex of museums and historic buildings in Memphis, Tennessee; its exhibits trace the history of the civil rights movement in the United States from the 17th century to the present.

Connection to Invisible Man

The museum is a chronicle of african american history and is a representation of the abuses wrongfully subjected to them. It shows some of the real life parallels of the racially motivated events in Invisible Man.
